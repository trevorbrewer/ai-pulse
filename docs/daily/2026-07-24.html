Researchers introduce VLM-IE3D, a framework designed to improve the 3D spatial reasoning capabilities of vision-language models that are typically trained on 2D image inputs. The system incorporates both implicit and explicit 3D geometric representations learned from RGB videos, without requiring dedicated depth sensors at inference time. The work addresses a known limitation of current VLMs, which often fail on tasks requiring fine-grained spatial understanding such as object localization in 3D space or scene geometry reasoning.

Expanding Flow MapsarXiv

A new paper introduces Expanding Generative Flows (EFlows), a flow-based generative modeling framework that can operate across distributions of varying dimensionality, unlike existing flow models that require fixed input and output dimensions. The method defines flows along an expanding interpolant, enabling generation of outputs with variable sequence lengths or spatial resolutions. This addresses a structural limitation of current flow-matching approaches, which are constrained to fixed-size state spaces, and could have implications for tasks like variable-length sequence generation or adaptive-resolution image synthesis.

GraphVid: Interactive Graph-Controllable Video GenerationarXiv

GraphVid proposes using scene graphs as control inputs for video generation, allowing users to specify multi-object interactions and spatial relationships without drawing per-object trajectory tracks. Existing trajectory-based video control methods scale poorly when multiple objects are present and become ambiguous under occlusion, problems the graph-based interface is designed to mitigate. Barzilai-Borwein Fails Superlinear Convergence on an Open Set of Quadratics for Every Dimension n≥4arXiv

A new theoretical result shows that the Barzilai-Borwein (BB) gradient method, widely used in large-scale optimization including machine learning training pipelines, does not achieve superlinear convergence on an open set of strictly convex quadratic problems for every finite dimension n≥4. This resolves a longstanding open question in optimization theory by providing a definitive negative answer, demonstrating that the strong practical performance of BB does not translate to superlinear theoretical guarantees. The finding has implications for understanding the limits of step-size adaptation strategies in gradient-based optimization.

Surprisal Theory is Tautological (without Rational Grounding)arXiv

A paper in computational linguistics argues that surprisal theory — the hypothesis that human reading difficulty for a word is an affine function of its surprisal under a language model — is mathematically tautological without additional constraints on which language model is used. The author demonstrates that for any non-negative difficulty measure, a language model exists whose surprisal is an affine function of it under mild technical conditions, meaning the theory is unfalsifiable in its current form. The argument calls for stronger rational grounding, specifically constraints on the language model, to give surprisal theory genuine empirical content.

Beyond Sufficiency: Time Series Explanation with Counterfactual NecessityarXiv

Researchers have introduced TimePNS, a method for explaining time-series classifiers that goes beyond identifying subsequences sufficient to preserve a model's prediction, requiring instead that those subsequences also be necessary for the decision. The distinction matters because existing sufficiency-oriented explainability methods can assign high importance scores to spurious subsequences that happen to support a prediction without being causally essential to it. TimePNS incorporates counterfactual necessity — asking whether removing a subsequence would change the model's output — as a formal criterion alongside sufficiency. The work addresses a known gap in interpretability for black-box time-series models, where faithfulness of explanations is difficult to guarantee with sufficiency alone.
